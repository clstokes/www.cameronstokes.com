Tasting: Lost Abbey Red Barn Ale

Jan 14th, 2011

Red Barn Ale is a Saison/Farmhouse spiced with Ginger, Orange Peel, Black Pepper and Grains of Paradise from Port Brewing/Lost Abbey based in San Marcos, California. Port Brewing/Lost Abbey has been around since the mid-90s and made a name for themselves making West-coast style beers under the Port Brewing name and Belgian-inspired and premium beers under Lost Abbey. Even though they’ve been around for over 15 years, they’re a new entrant to the craft beer market in Georgia making available beers under both names.

Beer Facts

Name: Red Barn Ale

Style: Belgian-style Saison

Brewery: Lost Abbey

Released: Occasional

Availability: 750 ml. bottles and draft

From the bottle:

Off in the distance, a Red Barn hides out content to do nothing more than was asked of it. Another summer day stretches out towards the crisp autumn nights and soon work will begin on another harvest. The barn relaxes knowing full well that each and every season, it will remain unmoved-yet touched by everything nature has to offer. And we’re left to wonder, does anyone notice or even care to stop and appreciate the beauty of the Red Barn? We did and so we brewed this lightly spiced farmhouse ale in honor of our favorite season-summer!

Hops: Phoenix and German Tettnang

Malts: Two Row, Flaked Wheat and Flaked Oats

Yeast: Belgian Saison Yeast

Adjuncts: Dextrose, Ginger, Orange Peel, Black Pepper and Grains of Paradise

OG: 1.065

FG: 1.012 IBUs: ?

ABV: 6.7%

Tasting

Serving: From a 750 ml. bottle into a Dogfish Head Signature Glass.

Appearance: Slightly opaque and orange in color. A careful pour leaves sediment in the bottom of the bottle. Has a pillowy, lingering head from constant carbonation being released. Clumpy lacing.

Aroma: Very effervescent. Spicy with orange zest. Alcohol in background.

Mouthfeel: Harsh carbonation at first, but mellows to the right amount. Light body. Any flavor or mouthfeel disappears after each sip.

Taste: Dry. Doesn’t taste as spicy as it smells. Bitter orange peel with a little ginger and black pepper sting in the back of the throat.

Conclusion

Red Barn Ale is a refreshing Saison and a great introduction to a new brewery. It doesn’t taste as spicy as it smells with a hint of ginger and black pepper but the orange peel is definitely there. I’ve never had Grains of Paradise so I can’t pick out that flavor. Red Barn Ale went great with Thai food.

I’m looking forward to trying other beers from Port Brewing/Lost Abbey.

Food pairings: Spicy Thai Food.

Cellar-able: Nah…

Comparable beers: Saison Dupont and Ommegang Hennepin.

Tasting: Cigar City Brewing Guava Grove

Jan 5th, 2011

Guava Grove is a Belgian-style Saison from Cigar City Brewing (CCB) in Tampa, Florida. Interestingly, CCB got somewhat of a start in Atlanta at the 2009 Atlanta Cask Ale Tasting when they took first place with a homebrewed version of their Hunaphu Imperial Stout. Since then, CCB has made quite a name for themselves churning out many surprising and innovative beers.

Beer Facts

Name: Guava Grove

Style: Belgian-style Saison

Brewery: Cigar City Brewing

Released: Seasonal in June/July

Availability: Seasonal in 750 ml. bottles and draft

Description:

Guava Grove is a Belgian-style ale that sees a secondary fermentation and extended aging on one of Tampa’s favored fruits, Guava. The complex flavors imparted by both the Belgian yeast strain and the Guava are unlike anything you are likely to encounter in other beers. The flavor is a complex weave of banana, guava, and tropical fruit with hints of clove. The elegant beer pairs well with fresh fish, mussels, fresh tropical fruits, and earthy cheeses. It also makes a fine accompaniment to Guavaween festivities.

Hops: ?

Malts: ?

OG: ?

FG: ?

IBUs: ?

ABV: 8.0%

Tasting

Serving: From a 750 ml. bottle into a Rogue Ales XS goblet.

Appearance: Pours a hazy, opaque orange. No head at all but light lacing. Bit of sediment in the bottom.

Aroma: Very tart. Strong Brettanomyces.

Mouthfeel: Light carbonation and mouthfeel, perfect for the beer.

Taste: Not as tart as the aroma suggests. Definite guava taste, also apricot, tangerine and tropic fruits. No alcohol presence.

Conclusion

I’ve always been impressed by Cigar City’s beers and Guava Grove does not disappoint. The beer is not too tart and therefore very refreshing to drink. There is a nice guava flavor but also a lot of apricot and tangerine. On my first sip I was reminded of an orange-tangerine juice.

One of my favorite desserts is a good Flemish or Flanders sour with a bowl of vanilla ice cream to balance the tart. Guava Grove is just shy of being too tart to require something to balance it. It’s such a light beer with no alcohol present, I feel I could drink Guava Grove with breakfast.

According to our resident Atlanta Beer Master, Cigar City Brewing will be distributing to Georgia in 2011. I can’t wait!

Food pairings: A light seafood dish, salad, or fruit salad.

Cellar-able: Given the flavors and alcohol percentage, Guava Grove would likely not deteriorate over time, but probably would not improve.

Comparable beers: Duchesse De Bourgogne and Monk’s Café Flemish Sour Red Ale.

Cross-Origin Resource Sharing and Apache Httpd

Dec 26th, 2010

After asking Amazon to support the W3C Cross-Origin Resource Sharing specification for the Route 53 APIs I decided to look into what it would take to implement the necessary headers in the Apache web server.

The specification describes several headers that need to be set, but we’ll only focus on the main header, Access-Control-Allow-Origin. Setting the header is easy; determining when to set it and what to set it to is the trickier part. After a bit of digging through the HTTP Server documentation I found the necessary configuration. Using mod_headers and mod_setenvif you can easily support dynamic headers to support cross-origin resource sharing.

Here’s the config:

Line 1 uses SetEnvIf to set an environment variable if the request attribute matches the regular expression. In this case we’re matching on the Origin header for example.com or example.org and if it matches, the AccessControlAllowOrigin environment variable is set to the value of the Origin header as specified by the $1 backreference.

Line 2 adds the Access-Control-Allow-Origin header to the response. The value is set to the AccessControlAllowOrigin environment variable from line 1. The env=AccessControlAllowOrigin directive means the header will only be added if the AccessControlAllowOrigin environment variable has been set.

The specification states you can support a wildcard value (*) for the Access-Control-Allow-Origin header or a comma-delimited list of domains, but with this configuration you can be specific in permitting access to individual domains without specifying everyone you’ve given access to. The HTTP access control documentation at the Mozilla Developer Network provides a great reference for how to use the cross-origin resource sharing functionality on the server and client-side.

Tasting: Dogfish Head My Antonia

Nov 28th, 2010

My Antonia was born from a collaboration with Dogfish Head’s founder Sam Calgione and Leonardo DiVencenzo of the Italian brewery Birra del Borgo. My Antonia is an imperial pilsner brewed using Dogfish Head’s now famous continuous hopping process where hops are introduced throughout the boil rather than at set intervals. The first batch was brewed in Italy with a limited import to The States. In 2010, Dogfish Head brewed their inaugural batch at the Dogfish Head brewery in Delaware. My tasting is from the U.S. version.

Beer Facts

Name: My Antonia

Style: Imperial Pilsner

Brewery: Dogfish Head Craft Brewery, Inc.

Released: June 2010

Availability: “Occasional rarity” in 750 ml. bottles and draft.

Description:

This here beer is a lagerific departure from our ale-centric brewery. A marriage of old world tradition and new world innovation, My Antonia is brewed with pilsner malt and continually-hopped with a blend of Noble and West Coast hops.

Hops: “Noble and West Coast hops”

Malts: “Pilsner malt”

OG: ???

FG: ???

IBUs: ???

ABV: 7.5%

Tasting

Serving: From a 750 ml. bottle into a Dogfish Head pint glass.

Appearance: Pours hazy with a couple fingers of frothy, off-white head. The head lingers for a long time and leaves a good amount of lacing.

Aroma: Has a classic Noble hop, pilsner aroma.

Mouthfeel: Initially has a strong carbonation bite that goes away after time and becomes more drinkable. Medium mouthfeel that leaves a cloying stickiness causing a lot of mouthwatering after every sip.

Taste: A crisp, clean pilsner flavor. Hoppy for a pilsner, even an imperial pilsner. More hop bitterness than hop flavor. A minor, malty sweetness. Slight alcohol in the finish.

Conclusion

My Antonia is another great offering from Dogfish Head. Compared to their other rarities and extreme beers it’s certainly tame, but is a good contrast for them. My Antonia falls right in line with the pilsner kick I’ve been on recently. Having said that, it’s not my favorite imperial pilsner, that’s currently reserved for Sweetwater Brewing Company’s Magnum IP. As of this review, My Antonia isn’t available in Georgia and I grabbed this bottle on a recent trip to Florida. I’ll certainly pick up another bottle when given the opportunity. Why Florida get’s this and Georgie doesn’t is beyond me…

Food pairings: Spicy Asian food.

Cellar-able: No, being a pilsner, this would not age well.

Comparable beers: Sweetwater Magnum IP and Sam Adams Hallertau Imperial Pilsner are two I’ve had.
